\section{Background to Study}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Overview of Bitcoin and Cryptocurrency Markets}

Bitcoin (BTC) is a decentralized digital currency introduced in 2008 by an anonymous entity known as Satoshi Nakamoto \cite{nakamoto2008}. Operating on a peer-to-peer network secured by blockchain technology, Bitcoin allows for trustless financial transactions without reliance on any central authority such as a bank or government. Since its inception, Bitcoin has grown from an experimental technology to one of the world's most widely traded financial assets, with a market capitalization that has at various points exceeded one trillion US dollars.

The cryptocurrency market is distinguished by several characteristics that set it apart from traditional financial markets. Unlike stock exchanges, cryptocurrency markets operate continuously, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, across the globe. This perpetual operation, combined with relatively limited market depth compared to traditional assets and the absence of circuit breakers, contributes to the extreme price volatility that characterizes Bitcoin's trading history. The asset has experienced multiple price cycles, including a rise to approximately \$20,000 in late 2017, a subsequent crash below \$4,000 in 2018, and a dramatic surge past \$60,000 in 2021.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

Accurate prediction of Bitcoin prices has significant financial and academic value. For individual investors and institutional traders, even marginal improvements in predictive accuracy can translate to substantial financial returns. From a scientific perspective, Bitcoin represents a challenging testbed for forecasting methods due to its non-stationary dynamics, high noise levels, and susceptibility to exogenous shocks such as regulatory announcements and macroeconomic events.

Traditional financial forecasting methods, including ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) and GARCH (Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) models, rely on assumptions of linearity and stationarity that are routinely violated by cryptocurrency price series. Deep learning models, which are capable of learning highly non-linear mappings from high-dimensional input spaces, have emerged as compelling alternatives for this task.

\subsection{Motivation}

This project is motivated by three key observations:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item The inherent complexity of Bitcoin price dynamics renders classical statistical models insufficient for reliable prediction.
    \item Advances in deep learning architectures, particularly recurrent networks and hybrid convolutional-recurrent models, have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on sequential financial data.
    \item The availability of high-quality historical OHLCV (Open, High, Low, Close, Volume) data through APIs such as Yahoo Finance makes it feasible to train and validate multiple deep learning models in a reproducible manner.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scope of the Project}

This project focuses on predicting the next-day closing price of Bitcoin (BTC-USD) using historical market data from January 2017 to April 2026. Three deep learning architectures are designed, implemented, and compared: a Deep Neural Network (DNN), a Long Short-Term Memory network (LSTM), and a hybrid CNN-LSTM model. The study evaluates each model's performance using standard regression metrics and demonstrates real-time inference capability.

\section{Literature Review}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Traditional Approaches to Financial Forecasting}

Time-series forecasting in finance has a long history rooted in econometric models. Box and Jenkins' ARIMA framework \cite{box2015} provided the dominant paradigm for several decades, modelling a time series as a function of its own lagged values and lagged forecast errors. While effective for stationary, linear processes, ARIMA models have been consistently shown to struggle with the highly volatile, non-linear dynamics of cryptocurrency prices.

GARCH-family models extended this framework to capture time-varying volatility, but their assumptions regarding the distributional form of innovations remain problematic for assets like Bitcoin, which exhibits heavy tails and skewness in its return distribution. Researchers such as Katsiampa \cite{katsiampa2017} evaluated various GARCH specifications for Bitcoin volatility but found that accurate point-price prediction remained elusive.

\subsection{Machine Learning in Financial Prediction}

The introduction of machine learning techniques opened new avenues for financial forecasting. Support Vector Regression (SVR), Random Forests, and Gradient Boosting methods were applied to Bitcoin prediction tasks with moderate success. Madan et al. \cite{madan2015} demonstrated that Random Forests and SVMs could outperform baseline models when applied to short-term Bitcoin price movements, though their inability to inherently model sequential dependencies remained a limitation.

\subsection{Deep Learning for Sequential Financial Data}

\subsubsection{Recurrent Neural Networks and LSTM}

The introduction of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber \cite{hochreiter1997} addressed the vanishing gradient problem that plagued standard recurrent networks, enabling effective learning of long-range temporal dependencies. LSTMs have since become the de facto standard for sequence modelling in finance.

Fischer and Krauss \cite{fischer2018} applied deep LSTMs to stock market prediction and demonstrated statistically significant outperformance over naive benchmarks. For Bitcoin specifically, Patel et al. \cite{patel2020} showed that stacked LSTM architectures could capture multi-day price momentum patterns that simpler models missed. The key advantage of LSTM lies in its gating mechanism: the forget gate, input gate, and output gate collectively decide which information to retain, update, or expose, enabling the network to selectively remember relevant historical patterns across sequences of 30 to 90 days.

\subsubsection{Convolutional Neural Networks for Time Series}

While Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) were originally developed for image recognition, their ability to detect local patterns via learned filters has proven valuable for time-series data. Applied to a one-dimensional price sequence, a CNN kernel functions analogously to a sliding template matcher, identifying recurring short-term patterns such as price spikes, consolidations, or trend reversals. Borovykh et al. \cite{borovykh2017} demonstrated that dilated causal convolutions could achieve competitive performance on financial time-series prediction tasks.

\subsubsection{Hybrid CNN-LSTM Models}

The most significant recent advance in financial time-series prediction has been the development of hybrid architectures that combine CNNs and LSTMs in a sequential pipeline. In such architectures, the CNN component acts as a local feature extractor, identifying short-term patterns in the raw price sequence, while the LSTM component subsequently models the long-range temporal relationships between these extracted features.

Livieris et al. \cite{livieris2020} applied a CNN-LSTM hybrid to gold price prediction and reported superior performance compared to pure CNN or pure LSTM baselines. Similar findings were reported for Bitcoin specifically by Kara and Gungor \cite{kara2022}, who demonstrated that the hybrid architecture's ability to simultaneously capture local and global temporal structure gave it a consistent advantage across multiple evaluation windows.

\subsection{Feature Engineering for Cryptocurrency Prediction}

Technical indicators derived from OHLCV data have been shown to enrich the feature space available to deep learning models. Moving averages (both simple and exponential), Relative Strength Index (RSI), Bollinger Bands, and momentum indicators have all been incorporated in prior work \cite{chen2020}. The rationale is that these indicators encode domain knowledge about trend direction, momentum, and overbought/oversold conditions that the raw price series alone does not convey.

\subsection{Research Gap and Contribution}

While prior work has evaluated individual architectures in isolation, direct comparisons of DNN, LSTM, and CNN-LSTM models trained on the same dataset with the same preprocessing pipeline are relatively rare. This project contributes a controlled, three-way comparative evaluation using consistent feature engineering, normalization, and evaluation protocols, thereby enabling fair attribution of performance differences to architectural choices rather than data preparation artifacts.

\section{Research Methodology}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Research Design}

This study adopts a quantitative, experimental research design. The central research question is: \textit{Which deep learning architecture---DNN, LSTM, or CNN-LSTM---produces the most accurate next-day Bitcoin closing price predictions when trained on historical OHLCV data augmented with technical indicators?}

To answer this question, a controlled experiment is conducted in which three model architectures are trained and evaluated under identical conditions: the same dataset, the same feature engineering pipeline, the same train/validation/test splits, the same optimizer, and the same loss function. Differences in evaluation metrics can therefore be attributed to the architectural properties of each model.

\subsection{Data Source and Collection}

Bitcoin historical price data (ticker symbol: BTC-USD) was retrieved from Yahoo Finance using the \texttt{yfinance} Python library. The dataset spans from January 1, 2017 to the present date at the time of training, covering approximately 2,600 trading days. Each observation includes six fields: Open, High, Low, Close, Volume, and Adjusted Close. The Adjusted Close column was dropped as it is identical to Close for cryptocurrencies (no dividend or split adjustments apply). All rows with missing values were removed prior to analysis.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

Ten input features were constructed from the raw OHLCV data:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Open}: The opening price for the trading day.
    \item \textbf{High}: The highest price reached during the day.
    \item \textbf{Low}: The lowest price reached during the day.
    \item \textbf{Close}: The closing price of the day (also the prediction target).
    \item \textbf{Volume}: Total trading volume in the day.
    \item \textbf{SMA\_7}: 7-day Simple Moving Average of the closing price.
    \item \textbf{SMA\_30}: 30-day Simple Moving Average of the closing price.
    \item \textbf{EMA\_14}: 14-day Exponential Moving Average, computed with \texttt{adjust=False}.
    \item \textbf{RSI\_14}: 14-day Relative Strength Index, computed as:
    \begin{equation}
        \text{RSI} = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + \frac{\overline{\text{gain}}}{\overline{\text{loss}} + \epsilon}}
    \end{equation}
    where $\overline{\text{gain}}$ and $\overline{\text{loss}}$ are rolling 14-day averages of upward and downward price changes, and $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$ prevents division by zero.
    \item \textbf{Daily\_Return}: Percentage change in closing price from the previous day, $r_t = (C_t - C_{t-1}) / C_{t-1}$.
\end{enumerate}

After engineering all features, rows containing \texttt{NaN} values (resulting from the 30-day rolling window) were discarded. The effective dataset begins approximately 30 days after January 1, 2017.

\subsection{Data Normalization}

All ten features were normalized to the range $[0, 1]$ using Min-Max Scaling:
\begin{equation}
    x_{\text{scaled}} = \frac{x - x_{\min}}{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}
\end{equation}
A separate \texttt{MinMaxScaler} was fitted exclusively on the Close price column to enable accurate inverse transformation of model predictions back to USD.

\subsection{Sequence Construction}

The dataset was transformed into a supervised learning format using a sliding window of length $T = 60$ days. Each input sample $X_i \in \mathbb{R}^{60 \times 10}$ consists of 60 consecutive days of feature vectors, and the corresponding target $y_i \in \mathbb{R}$ is the normalized Close price on day $i$. This formulation is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{C}_{t+1} = f\left( \mathbf{X}_{t-59}, \mathbf{X}_{t-58}, \ldots, \mathbf{X}_{t} \right)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Train/Validation/Test Split}

Data was split chronologically (without shuffling, to preserve temporal order):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Training set}: 70\% of sequences (earliest dates)
    \item \textbf{Validation set}: 15\% of sequences (middle period)
    \item \textbf{Test set}: 15\% of sequences (most recent dates)
\end{itemize}
This split ensures that the test set represents genuinely unseen future data, simulating the realistic deployment scenario.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Three standard regression metrics were used to evaluate and compare model performance on the held-out test set:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)}: Penalizes large prediction errors more heavily.
    \begin{equation}
        \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\hat{y}_i - y_i)^2}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Mean Absolute Error (MAE)}: The average absolute difference between predicted and actual prices.
    \begin{equation}
        \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|\hat{y}_i - y_i|
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Coefficient of Determination ($R^2$)}: Proportion of variance in the target explained by the model.
    \begin{equation}
        R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\hat{y}_i - y_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \bar{y})^2}
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

All metrics were computed on predictions that were inverse-transformed back to the original USD price scale.

\section{Proposed Solution}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{System Architecture Overview}

The proposed system is a multi-model deep learning pipeline for next-day Bitcoin price prediction. The pipeline consists of five stages:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Data Acquisition}: Live OHLCV data retrieval from Yahoo Finance.
    \item \textbf{Feature Engineering}: Construction of technical indicators to enrich the input signal.
    \item \textbf{Preprocessing}: Min-Max normalization and sliding-window sequence construction.
    \item \textbf{Model Training}: Parallel training of DNN, LSTM, and CNN-LSTM architectures.
    \item \textbf{Inference}: Real-time prediction of the next-day closing price using saved model weights.
\end{enumerate}

The rationale for deploying three distinct architectures is that each captures a different inductive bias that may be better suited to certain regimes of the Bitcoin price series. A DNN captures complex static relationships within a flattened feature vector; an LSTM is explicitly designed for sequential data and can learn long-range dependencies; and a CNN-LSTM hybrid combines short-term pattern extraction with long-range dependency modelling.

\subsection{Model 1: Deep Neural Network (DNN)}

The DNN flattens the 60-day input sequence into a single feature vector of dimensionality $60 \times 10 = 600$ and applies a stack of fully-connected layers with non-linear activations. The proposed architecture is:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Input (600)} \rightarrow \text{FC(512)} \rightarrow \text{BN} \rightarrow \text{ReLU} \rightarrow \text{Dropout(0.3)}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \rightarrow \text{FC(256)} \rightarrow \text{BN} \rightarrow \text{ReLU} \rightarrow \text{Dropout(0.3)}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \rightarrow \text{FC(128)} \rightarrow \text{BN} \rightarrow \text{ReLU} \rightarrow \text{Dropout(0.2)} \rightarrow \text{FC(64)} \rightarrow \text{ReLU} \rightarrow \text{FC(1)}
\end{equation}

Batch Normalization (BN) is applied after each major linear layer to stabilize training dynamics, reduce internal covariate shift, and accelerate convergence. Dropout layers randomly zero out activations during training at rates of 0.3 and 0.2, acting as a regularizer to mitigate overfitting. The final linear layer produces a single scalar output representing the predicted normalized closing price.

\subsection{Model 2: Long Short-Term Memory Network (LSTM)}

The LSTM model accepts the full 3-dimensional input tensor of shape $(B, 60, 10)$ where $B$ is the batch size, preserving the temporal ordering of the feature vectors. The LSTM cell updates its hidden state according to the following gating equations at each time step $t$:

\begin{align}
    f_t &= \sigma(W_f \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f) \quad \text{(forget gate)} \\
    i_t &= \sigma(W_i \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i) \quad \text{(input gate)} \\
    \tilde{c}_t &= \tanh(W_c \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_c) \quad \text{(candidate cell)} \\
    c_t &= f_t \odot c_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{c}_t \quad \text{(cell state update)} \\
    o_t &= \sigma(W_o \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o) \quad \text{(output gate)} \\
    h_t &= o_t \odot \tanh(c_t) \quad \text{(hidden state)}
\end{align}

The proposed LSTM architecture uses 2 stacked layers with a hidden size of 128 and dropout of 0.3 between layers. Only the hidden state at the final time step ($h_{60}$) is passed to a two-layer fully-connected head:
\begin{equation}
    h_{60} \rightarrow \text{FC(64)} \rightarrow \text{ReLU} \rightarrow \text{Dropout(0.2)} \rightarrow \text{FC(1)}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Model 3: Hybrid CNN-LSTM}

The CNN-LSTM architecture is the most sophisticated of the three. It processes the input in two sequential stages:

\textbf{Stage 1 --- CNN Feature Extraction:} The input tensor is permuted to shape $(B, 10, 60)$ to treat the 10 features as input channels and the 60 time steps as the spatial dimension for 1D convolution. Two convolutional blocks are applied:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Conv1D}(10 \rightarrow 64, k=3) \rightarrow \text{BN} \rightarrow \text{ReLU} \rightarrow \text{MaxPool1D}(2)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \rightarrow \text{Conv1D}(64 \rightarrow 128, k=3) \rightarrow \text{BN} \rightarrow \text{ReLU} \rightarrow \text{Dropout}(0.3)
\end{equation}
MaxPooling with kernel size 2 reduces the sequence length from 60 to 30, compressing the temporal resolution while retaining the most salient local features.

\textbf{Stage 2 --- LSTM Temporal Modelling:} The CNN output, of shape $(B, 30, 128)$, is permuted back and fed into a 2-layer LSTM with hidden size 128. The final hidden state is passed to a fully-connected head identical to that of the pure LSTM model.

The CNN-LSTM's dual capacity to detect local price patterns (via convolutions) and model long-range temporal dependencies (via LSTM) makes it theoretically the most capable architecture for this task.

\subsection{Training Strategy}

All three models share a common training strategy:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Optimizer}: Adam with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and weight decay $10^{-5}$.
    \item \textbf{Loss Function}: Mean Squared Error (MSE).
    \item \textbf{Batch Size}: 64.
    \item \textbf{Maximum Epochs}: 100.
    \item \textbf{Learning Rate Scheduling}: ReduceLROnPlateau with patience 7 and reduction factor 0.5.
    \item \textbf{Early Stopping}: Training halts if validation loss does not improve for 15 consecutive epochs; the best model weights are restored.
    \item \textbf{Gradient Clipping}: Gradient norms are clipped to a maximum of 1.0 to prevent exploding gradients.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{DNN Implementation}

The DNN was implemented as a \texttt{torch.nn.Module} using \texttt{nn.Sequential}:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption=DNN Architecture]
class DeepNeuralNetwork(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.model = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Flatten(),
            nn.Linear(input_dim, 512), nn.BatchNorm1d(512),
            nn.ReLU(), nn.Dropout(0.3),
            nn.Linear(512, 256), nn.BatchNorm1d(256),
            nn.ReLU(), nn.Dropout(0.3),
            nn.Linear(256, 128), nn.BatchNorm1d(128),
            nn.ReLU(), nn.Dropout(0.2),
            nn.Linear(128, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(64, 1)
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.model(x)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{LSTM Implementation}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption=LSTM Architecture]
class LSTMModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_size, hidden_size=128, num_layers=2):
        super().__init__()
        self.lstm = nn.LSTM(input_size, hidden_size,
                            num_layers, dropout=0.3,
                            batch_first=True)
        self.fc = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_size, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Dropout(0.2), nn.Linear(64, 1)
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        out, _ = self.lstm(x)
        return self.fc(out[:, -1, :])
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{CNN-LSTM Implementation}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption=CNN-LSTM Architecture]
class CNNLSTMModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_size, seq_len, hidden_size=128):
        super().__init__()
        self.cnn = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Conv1d(input_size, 64, kernel_size=3, padding=1),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(64), nn.ReLU(), nn.MaxPool1d(2),
            nn.Conv1d(64, 128, kernel_size=3, padding=1),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(128), nn.ReLU(), nn.Dropout(0.3)
        )
        self.lstm = nn.LSTM(128, hidden_size, 2,
                            dropout=0.3, batch_first=True)
        self.fc = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_size, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Dropout(0.2), nn.Linear(64, 1)
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        x = self.cnn(x.permute(0, 2, 1)).permute(0, 2, 1)
        out, _ = self.lstm(x)
        return self.fc(out[:, -1, :])
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Training Loop}

A unified \texttt{train\_model()} function was used for all three architectures. Key implementation details include the use of \texttt{torch.no\_grad()} context during validation, gradient clipping via \texttt{nn.utils.clip\_grad\_norm\_()}, and restoration of the best validation-loss checkpoint upon early stopping. DataLoaders were constructed with \texttt{shuffle=False} to preserve temporal ordering in all splits.

\subsection{Real-Time Inference}

After training, all three models were serialized to disk using \texttt{torch.save(model.state\_dict(), ...)}. An inference pipeline was implemented that fetches the most recent 90 days of live BTC-USD data, re-engineers the same ten features, normalizes using the already-fitted training scalers (transform-only, no re-fitting), constructs the final 60-day sequence, and runs all three models to produce individual predictions. An ensemble prediction is computed as the arithmetic mean of the three model outputs. The pipeline also computes and displays the predicted price change and percentage change relative to the most recent known closing price, providing an actionable summary for the end user.

\section{Evaluation}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Training Dynamics}

All three models were trained for up to 100 epochs with early stopping. The loss curves for training and validation sets were plotted for each model, allowing visual inspection of convergence behaviour.

The DNN exhibited relatively fast initial convergence but showed signs of overfitting, with the validation loss plateauing while the training loss continued to decrease, before early stopping intervened. This behaviour is consistent with the DNN's lack of any explicit sequential inductive bias—by flattening the 60-day window, it treats temporally ordered features as an unordered bag of inputs, potentially overfitting to spurious patterns in the training set.

The LSTM demonstrated smoother, more stable convergence, with training and validation loss tracking closely for a greater number of epochs before diverging. This suggests that the LSTM's recurrent structure provides a better inductive bias for this inherently sequential task, reducing its tendency to overfit relative to the DNN.

The CNN-LSTM exhibited the smoothest convergence of the three models, with consistently low and well-tracked training and validation losses. The CNN's local feature extraction appeared to provide a beneficial regularization effect by compressing the 60-day sequence to 30 steps before LSTM processing, reducing the effective sequence length and lowering the risk of the LSTM overfitting to noise in the raw price series.

The prediction visualization plot overlays actual Bitcoin closing prices against the predictions of all three models for the test period. Several consistent patterns were observed:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item All three models track the general trend of the Bitcoin price series with high fidelity, reflecting the strong autocorrelation in daily closing prices.
    \item During periods of high volatility---sharp spikes or crashes---all models exhibit a characteristic one-day lag, predicting today's price rather than tomorrow's movement. This ``smoothed prediction'' effect is common in financial deep learning models and reflects the difficulty of anticipating sudden regime changes.
    \item The LSTM and CNN-LSTM models generally show tighter alignment with local price movements compared to the DNN, consistent with their superior ability to model temporal dependencies.
    \item The CNN-LSTM predictions tend to be the closest to the actual values during high-volatility periods, suggesting that the CNN's short-term pattern extraction provides a meaningful advantage in capturing local dynamics.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Comparison}

The comparative bar charts across RMSE, MAE, and $R^2$ support the following ranked ordering of model performance:
\begin{equation}
    \text{CNN-LSTM} > \text{LSTM} > \text{DNN}
\end{equation}

This ranking is consistent with the theoretical expectations: the CNN-LSTM's dual-stage architecture allows it to exploit both local and global structure in the price series, giving it an advantage over the pure LSTM (which lacks local feature extraction) and the DNN (which lacks sequential inductive bias entirely).

The DNN's competitive performance despite its architectural limitations highlights the strong predictability of next-day Bitcoin prices from a 60-day historical window when appropriate technical indicators are included. The high correlation among price-level features means that even a static model can approximate the conditional expectation reasonably well, particularly during trend-following market phases.

\subsection{Real-Time Prediction Results}

The real-time inference pipeline successfully retrieved live BTC-USD data, engineered the required features, and produced next-day price predictions from all three models. The ensemble prediction (arithmetic mean of DNN, LSTM, and CNN-LSTM outputs) provides a more robust estimate than any single model alone, as individual model biases partially cancel in the average.

The visualization of the last 90 days of actual prices alongside the four prediction points (DNN, LSTM, CNN-LSTM, Ensemble) provides an intuitive representation of model agreement and uncertainty. When the three individual predictions are closely clustered, ensemble confidence is high; when they diverge significantly, it signals increased predictive uncertainty.

\subsection{Discussion of Limitations}

Several important limitations should be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Market Regime Changes}: All three models are trained on a static dataset. The Bitcoin market has undergone structural changes (increased institutional participation, ETF approvals, macroeconomic coupling) that may reduce the relevance of earlier historical patterns.
    \item \textbf{Exogenous Factors}: The models use only price and volume data. Factors such as regulatory news, on-chain metrics, sentiment from social media, or macroeconomic indicators are not included, limiting the models' ability to anticipate event-driven price movements.
    \item \textbf{One-Day Lag}: The characteristic prediction lag during high-volatility periods limits the models' usefulness for active trading strategies during market-moving events.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation on Historical Data}: Strong $R^2$ scores on historical test data do not guarantee profitable trading performance in live markets, where transaction costs, slippage, and market impact must be considered.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

This project designed, implemented, and evaluated three deep learning architectures---a Deep Neural Network, a Long Short-Term Memory Network, and a hybrid CNN-LSTM model---for the task of next-day Bitcoin closing price prediction. Using approximately 2,600 days of historical BTC-USD data from Yahoo Finance, augmented with seven technical indicators (SMA\_7, SMA\_30, EMA\_14, RSI\_14, Daily Return), and a 60-day lookback window, each model was trained under identical conditions to enable fair architectural comparison.

The experimental results support the following conclusions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{All three models demonstrated meaningful predictive capability}, achieving high $R^2$ scores and tracking the general trend of Bitcoin prices on the unseen test set. This confirms the feasibility of deep learning for short-term cryptocurrency price forecasting.
    \item \textbf{The CNN-LSTM hybrid outperformed both baselines} on RMSE, MAE, and $R^2$, validating the hypothesis that combining local feature extraction (CNN) with long-range temporal modelling (LSTM) provides a superior inductive bias for financial time-series prediction.
    \item \textbf{The LSTM outperformed the DNN}, confirming the importance of preserving temporal sequence structure rather than flattening the input, even when augmented with domain-specific technical indicators.
    \item \textbf{Feature engineering substantially enriches the input signal}. Technical indicators encode domain knowledge about trend direction, momentum, and market conditions that the raw OHLCV series alone does not convey, enabling all three models to achieve competitive performance.
    \item \textbf{The real-time inference pipeline} demonstrated the practical applicability of the trained models, producing live next-day price predictions from the most recent available market data without requiring model retraining.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Recommendations for Future Work}

Several directions could further improve the system's predictive accuracy and practical utility:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Transformer-based architectures}: Multi-head self-attention mechanisms, as in the Temporal Fusion Transformer \cite{lim2021}, have shown state-of-the-art results on multivariate time-series forecasting and could be compared against the CNN-LSTM baseline.
    \item \textbf{Alternative data integration}: Incorporating on-chain metrics (hash rate, active addresses, exchange flows), social media sentiment, and macroeconomic indicators could address the exogenous factor limitation identified above.
    \item \textbf{Probabilistic forecasting}: Replacing point predictions with distributional outputs (e.g., via Monte Carlo Dropout or Bayesian neural networks) would provide prediction intervals, giving end users a quantitative measure of forecast uncertainty.
    \item \textbf{Online learning}: Implementing rolling retraining or continual learning to adapt model weights to recent market data would reduce the risk of performance degradation under distribution shift.
    \item \textbf{Multi-step ahead prediction}: Extending the output horizon from one day to five or ten days would provide more actionable signals for medium-term portfolio management.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Final Remarks}

This study demonstrates that deep learning, and particularly the CNN-LSTM hybrid architecture, provides a powerful framework for Bitcoin price prediction. The combination of principled feature engineering, appropriate model design, and rigorous evaluation methodology produces a system that captures meaningful patterns in one of the world's most volatile financial markets. While the models are not intended as financial advice and carry inherent limitations, they represent a meaningful contribution to the growing body of evidence supporting deep learning as a productive tool for cryptocurrency market analysis.
